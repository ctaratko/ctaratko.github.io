Introduction

Current households face the question of whether or not they should spend money to build up their assets, more specifically their long term assets. Long Term Assets is a broad classification of assets, but when specified under a Household, it normally pertains to assets such as:

  • Houses

  • Car

  • Small Businesses

the US consumer body as a whole is at a state where its becoming less and less common to have a larger amount of debt relative to one’s income, as as financially sound this is, it also creates concern for future innovation. Entrepreneurship requires risks to be taken, and the US has been the global powerhouse for innovation and research for new products and services, and the US government has backed this innovation with the amount of debt that’s been acquired.

As of recently, it is able to be seen that individuals have began choosing new methods of acquiring debt, but for less innovative or valued purposes, but instead for financially illiterate purposes. It is common for people to overspend on their line(s) of revolving credit (credit cards) that have balances due at the end of each month, or else they’ll be charged interest on the balance. Other options are people will over-spend on their debit card, and end up utilizing methods such as “Buy-Now-Pay-Later”, or “Pay-Day” Loans.

Empirical Strategy / Methods

Pooled OLS Model

\[\begin{aligned} \log(DTI\_ratio_{it}) =\;& \beta_1 \log(DebtValue_{it}) + \beta_2 \log(Median\_H\_Inc_{it}) + \beta_3 MinWage_{it} \\ &+ \beta_4 \log(HPI_{it}) + \beta_5 Delinquency\_pct_{it} + \beta_6 Med\_Mpct_{it} \\ &+ \beta_7 \log(mean\_CPI_{it}) + \beta_8 ForeclosureRate_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned}\]

Fixed Effect Model

\[\begin{aligned} \log(DTI\_ratio_{it}) =\;& \beta_1 \log(DebtValue_{it}) + \beta_2 \log(Median\_H\_Inc_{it}) + \beta_3 MinWage_{it} \\ &+ \beta_4 \log(HPI_{it}) + \beta_5 Delinquency\_pct_{it} + \beta_6 Med\_Mpct_{it} \\ &+ \beta_7 \log(mean\_CPI_{it}) + \beta_8 ForeclosureRate_{it} + \alpha_i + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned}\]

Interpretation:

  • \(\alpha_i\): captures all time-invariant state characteristics

Two-Way Fixed Effect Model

\[\begin{aligned} \log(DTI\_ratio_{it}) =\;& \beta_1 \log(DebtValue_{it}) + \beta_2 \log(Median\_H\_Inc_{it}) + \beta_3 MinWage_{it} \\ &+ \beta_4 \log(HPI_{it}) + \beta_5 Delinquency\_pct_{it} + \beta_6 Med\_Mpct_{it} \\ &+ \beta_7 \log(mean\_CPI_{it}) + \beta_8 ForeclosureRate_{it} + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{it} \end{aligned}\]

Interpretation:

  • \(\alpha_i\)​: state fixed effects
  • \(\gamma_t\)​: time fixed effects (e.g., macro shocks)

Results

According to all three plots, there is high statistical significance from majority of the variables within each model. Starting off with Fixed effect, all variables are statistically significant to the model. As for Two-Way FE, the variables that lack statistical significance in the Delinquency Percent, Foreclosure Rate, and Minimum Wage. Lastly, with the Pooled OLS, all variables hold statistical significance except for the constant.

Key notes to be taken about specific variables would be the \(\log(DebtValue)\), which is always negative and highly significant, ranging between -1.05 and -1.21. For a 1% increase in the total debt is associated with about 1.05% - 1.21% decrease within the Debt-to-Income Ratio. As for the next significant variable, \(\log(Median Household Income)\), has stronger effects within the Fixed-Effect & Pooled OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) Model. With the opposite effect of the DebtValue, it’s positive relationship has an effect of 1% increase in Median Household Income resulting in an increase of the Debt-to-Income Ratio by ~0.75% - 1.07%.

Discussion and Implications

Public Policy

There are many public policies that can be implemented when it comes to the results of the data. Starting off, Income based debt relief programs are a very easy and direct form of assisting public financial health. While this only solves the problem in the Short Run, it does allow individuals to be given a second chance on debt they would’ve otherwise been unable to pay back. For the second option, there’s the use of Financial Literacy Courses. Courses like these would have the opportunity to be implemented within any school, preferably targeted towards High-School students that are close to graduating. While this can be effective for teaching individuals who will begin facing the use of debt, it would also be effective to offer programs to individuals that are already established in their career or outside of primary schooling. This would be the most beneficial, allowing people to get practice with financial situations they may come across, and create a baseline of financial literacy to allow people to know their limits in relation to their finances and the economy overall. Lastly, a viable public policy would be to implement a sub division of the Federal Housing Finance Agency, one that would would create a financial database of understanding housing valuations via the specifications of the houses. Starting points would consist of County/State locations, House Age, House-owned Appliances retained through sale of the home, and ratings of local education.

Business Implications

Businesses play an integral role in terms of Household Financial Health. Individuals rely on larger institutions to provide loans and the terms to be agreeable on both ends of the contract. With that said, companies are able to provide different loans, such as “Pay-Day” loans, or even “Buy-Now Pay-Later” Structures that are sold through third parties, but backed by large financial institutions. These structures are easy ways to get access to money or to even stretch payments over a period of time. The issue that presents itself here is the background checks and the usage of these financial instruments. These instruments can be used on non-essentials, and have less restrictions state-by-state in terms of capping their interest rates & debt collection. These loans aren’t FDIC backed, but have large financial institutions putting forth equity on them, despite their debt collection being minimally invasive in relation to delinquent payments. This implies that the debt on these loans are utilized on the books for their high interest rates to be collected in the future. These are high risk investments due to their lack of debt collection, but the issue is significant due to it being monetarily backed by Financial Institutions. This can cause increase in rates on normal loans to offset the risk of this investment, but could cause macro-economic issues if the monetary value is large enough. Overall, the business implication from this data is to regulate these financial instruments in order to balance the incentive of utilizing backed, standardized financial instruments with lower risk.

Conclusion

Debt-to-Income ratios are based on the premise that individuals and entities can only acquire so much debt until they become over-burdened. Companies are known for higher DTI ratios due to their ability to continuously generate profit as long as there’s no significant likelihood for Going Concern. Some companies are more debt heavy than others, usually pertaining to specific Sectors or Industries of the market. The same relates to individuals and households, just at lower levels.

The Regression table estimates from pooled OLS, state fixed effects (FE), and two-way fixed effects (TWFE) models. Across all specifications, debt value and housing prices exhibit statistically significant negative relationships with the debt-to-income ratio, while median household income is consistently positive and significant. The magnitude of these coefficients remains relatively stable across models, suggesting robust relationships.

However, several variables—such as minimum wage, delinquency rates, and foreclosure rates—lose statistical significance in the TWFE specification. This indicates that their apparent effects in simpler models are likely driven by time-specific shocks rather than within-state variation. In particular, the coefficient on CPI changes sign when time fixed effects are included, highlighting the importance of controlling for macroeconomic trends.

Overall, the differences between the FE and TWFE models suggest that unobserved time effects play a substantial role in explaining variation in the debt-to-income ratio, and failure to account for these effects may lead to biased estimates.
